# Market — Zimbabwe / Southern Africa

The market is Zimbabwe first, Southern Africa second. Every post must read as
if written by someone who farms here — not translated in from a US template.

## Language & units (law)
- **Spelling:** British / South African. *fertiliser, maize, tonne, colour,
  labour, programme, realise, neighbour, centre, analyse.* Never *fertilizer,
  corn, ton, color.*
- **"Maize", never "corn".** This is the fastest tell of a foreign account.
- **Currency: USD.** The working currency of Zimbabwean agriculture. Show
  prices, margins and costs in US$. Mention ZWL only if a post is specifically
  about local-currency dynamics.
- **Units:** hectares (ha), tonnes (t), kg, mm of rainfall, °C. Metric always.
- **Dates/seasons:** the Southern-Hemisphere calendar. Summer cropping ≈
  Nov–Apr; the rains arrive ~Nov; harvest ≈ Apr–Jun; dry/winter season ≈
  May–Aug (wheat under irrigation).

## Real furniture (use correctly, never as decoration)
- **Crops:** maize (staple + commercial), tobacco (flue-cured, the big export),
  soyabeans, groundnuts, cotton, sorghum & small grains (millet, rapoko),
  sunflower, horticulture (tomatoes, leafy veg), sugar beans.
- **Seasons/rhythm:** first planting rains, tasselling, the green season, the
  lean season, market days.
- **Institutions & terms:** Agritex (extension officers), GMB (Grain Marketing
  Board), TIMB (tobacco board), contract/side-marketing, mukando / rounds
  (rotating savings), inputs schemes, communal vs A1 vs A2 vs commercial farms,
  Natural Regions I–V (agro-ecological zones; III–V are the drier, riskier ones).
- **Climate reality:** rain-fed dependence, erratic onset, mid-season dry
  spells, recurrent El Niño drought. Resilience is not abstract here.

## Agro-ecological grounding
Zimbabwe's Natural Regions I–V run from high-rainfall (I) to arid (V). ~80% of
the rural population is in III–V, where dryland farming is a genuine gamble.
When a post talks risk, water or timing, it should implicitly respect which
region the reader is likely in.

## Saviour-frame ban (hard law)
Never position KurimaSense as rescuing, empowering, uplifting, or bringing
technology "to" African farmers. Our audience are skilled operators running
hard businesses in tough conditions. We hand them a sharper instrument. Tone is
peer-to-peer expert, never benefactor-to-beneficiary.

**Banned frames:** "empowering smallholders", "bringing precision ag to Africa",
"lifting farmers out of poverty", "the future of African farming is here",
"tech for good in the bush", any white-saviour or poverty-porn imagery.

## Imagery rules (ties to Phase 6 image layer)
- Real Southern-African farming contexts. No US cornfields, no palm-fringed
  paddies, no conical straw hats standing in for "Africa".
- Dignified, working, capable — operators, not victims.
- Abstract/anonymous where people appear (brand-safety, Phase 6): no public
  figures, no identifiable individuals used as endorsement.

## Localisation checklist (run on every draft)
- [ ] Maize (not corn), British spelling throughout.
- [ ] Money in USD.
- [ ] Metric units, Southern-Hemisphere season.
- [ ] Any crop/institution named is real and used correctly.
- [ ] No saviour framing, no US-default assumptions.
- [ ] Imagery reads as Southern Africa, dignified and working.
